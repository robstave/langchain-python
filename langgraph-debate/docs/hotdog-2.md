[Moderator] Proposition:
  "A hot dog is a sandwich."

════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
[Pro] Round 1
Well now, if it please the court, let me begin with a plain truth as steady as a church bell on Sunday mornin’: a sandwich is, at bottom, filling between bread. That’s the skeleton of the thing, the bones under the skin, and you can dress it up in fancy city clothes all you want, but you can’t change what it is. A hot dog is a savory filling nestled inside a sliced bread roll—one piece of bread hinged or split, yes sir, but bread all the same, holdin’ meat in a neat, hand-held package. By the ordinary meaning of the words, by the common custom of decent folks from here to kingdom come, that dog most certainly hunts as a sandwich.

Now, my learned friend may prance around talkin’ about “special categories” and “hot dog exceptionals,” but bless your heart, an exception ain’t a law of nature. We don’t quit callin’ a bird a bird just because it flies funny, and we don’t stop callin’ a sandwich a sandwich because its bread is joined at one side. If a sub, a po’ boy, or a meatball grinder can ride under the sandwich banner, then a hot dog, perched noble as a crow on a fencepost, belongs in that same family. The structure is right, the function is right, and the eating of it is right. So I submit to you, Your Honor, that in the grand and orderly world of edible things, a hot dog is indeed a sandwich—plain as day, and twice as satisfying.

[Con] Round 1
Whoa, whoa, hold on there, Counselor — you can’t just wave your hands and say “filling between bread” like dat solves everythin’. By that logic, a taco’s a sandwich, a burrito’s a sandwich, and a slice of lasagna is just a real committed sandwich if you squint hard enough. Come on now. A hot dog bun ain’t a normal roll split clean through; it’s a purpose-built cradle. That’s the whole point — it’s designed to hold one specific thing, in one specific way. A sandwich got two distinct pieces of bread makin’ a top and bottom. A hot dog bun is one connected piece, like a little canoe with the side ripped open. Big difference, even if you’re tryin’ to talk fancy about “common custom.”

And don’t get me started on this “if a sub, po’ boy, or grinder counts, then hot dog counts” nonsense. That argument is like sayin’ because a sedan and a pickup both got wheels, my uncle’s shopping cart is a car. Nah. Subs and grinders are just long sandwiches — still two sides, still bread structured like a sandwich. A hot dog is its own category, same way a bagel with lox is not a salad just because it’s got stuff on it. People call it a hot dog because that’s what it is. If the name, the construction, and the way folks eat it all point to “hot dog,” then maybe the law oughta listen to common sense for once. Oh, and one more thing — if you gotta stretch this hard to make it fit, that’s usually your answer right there.

[Judge] Round 1: Pro 6/10, Con 8/10
  CON wins because it better exposes the flaws in Pro’s overbroad definition and gives a stronger common-sense account of why a hot dog is its own category.

[Pro] Round 2
Well now, Your Honor, my learned friend would have y’all believe that because a hot dog bun is connected on one side, the whole lawful enterprise falls apart like a screen door in a hurricane. But that dog won’t hunt. The essence of a sandwich is not some ritual incantation about “two separate pieces” waved around like a church fan in August; it is bread surrounding or supporting a filling meant to be eaten by hand. A split roll, a hinged bun, a sub roll, a hoagie, a po’ boy—these are kinfolk under the same broad roof. The law, and common usage alike, do not require us to pretend every sandwich must be cut into perfect twin slabs like a hotel club sandwich at a bridal luncheon. If the bread functions as a vessel for meat and toppings, if it encloses the filling in a portable form, then the thing has the very bones of a sandwich, bless its heart.

And as for my learned friend’s talk of tacos, burritos, and lasagna—well, that’s a fine bit of scarecrow lawyering, if I do declare. We are not saying every food with stuff inside bread or near bread is a sandwich; we are saying a hot dog is, by its structure and use, a bread-and-filling assembly in the sandwich family. A taco uses a tortilla, not bread; a burrito is wrapped, not housed; and lasagna, well, that’s just a casserole with ambitions. But a hot dog sits in a bun, held in the hand, with the bread serving the filling just as surely as a sub or grinder does. Folks have long treated these items as part of the same edible category when they’re defining by function instead of by fancy labels. So if the court is fixin’ to be faithful to ordinary language, culinary reality, and the sensible ways people actually eat, then a hot dog is a sandwich—plain, practical, and proven.

[Con] Round 2
Whoa, hold on now — you keep sayin’ “purpose-built cradle” like that’s some magical legal doctrine. Excuse me, Your Honor, but a bun is a bun. If it’s bread and it’s holdin’ meat, we ain’t in the Smithsonian of Culinary Anthropology, we’re in the real world. A submarine sandwich and a hot dog are both bread with stuff in it. The fact that one got a cute little hinge instead of two separate slices don’t suddenly make it a different species. My uncle Angie had a sandwich press that worked the same way: one piece of bread, folded over, still a sandwich. You don’t get to invent a new category just ‘cause the bread got a seam in a funny place.

And the taco-burrito-lasagna thing? C’mon, that’s scarecrow lawyering with extra gravy. A taco uses a tortilla, not bread; a burrito is wrapped, not split; lasagna don’t even pretend to be handheld. But a hot dog? Handheld, bread vessel, filling inside. That’s the exact neighborhood a sandwich lives in. You’re tryin’ to draw a line where the public don’t draw one — and the public, the guy at the ballpark, the kid at the counter, they know a portable bread-and-filling situation when they see one. So yeah, call it a hot dog if you want, but don’t stand there and tell me it escaped the sandwich family. That dog still hunts.

[Judge] Round 2: Pro 8/10, Con 7/10
  Pro gave the cleaner, more principled definition of sandwich and did a better job handling the counterexamples, while Con was punchy but mostly just restated the claim.

[Pro] Round 3
Well now, Your Honor, my learned friend keeps circlin’ the barn and actin’ like the hinge in the bun is some kind of constitutional amendment to the Laws of Lunch. But bless his heart, that’s just cookin’ the books. The question ain’t whether a hot dog is *identical* to every other sandwich, nor whether it wears a three-piece suit like a club sandwich at Sunday dinner. The question is whether it fits the plain, ordinary, functional meaning of a sandwich: bread with a filling, made to be held in the hand and eaten as one portable unit. By that standard, a hot dog is squarely in the family, same as a sub, a hoagie, or a po’ boy—different cousins, same kin. A bun is bread; a frank is filling; the whole contraption exists to cradle meat in edible transport. If that don’t make a sandwich, then my dear departed Mama’s ham biscuits had better lawyer up.

Now my learned friend says the public “don’t draw that line,” but I must say, that claim is about as sturdy as a screen door in a windstorm. Folks absolutely do classify by structure and function when it suits common speech: open-faced sandwiches, tea sandwiches, subs, and grinders all live under the broad sandwich canopy because the law of language is not so fussy as to require two separate slabs of bread like twin pillars at the temple. And that little “purpose-built cradle” argument? Well, so what? A taco shell is purpose-built too, and nobody in this courtroom is foolish enough to say a special container means it ceases to be food with a filling. My learned friend wants a bright line where none exists, but the common-sense line is plain as a dirt road at noon: if it’s bread supporting savory contents in a handheld form, it walks like a sandwich and quacks like a sandwich, then it is a sandwich. So I submit, Your Honor, let the record reflect the truth—hot dog and all—it’s a sandwich, and that dog still hunts.

[Con] Round 3
Aw, c’mon now, Your Honor, this is where my learned friend starts doin’ the old “if it looks like bread, it’s bread enough” two-step. But he’s smudgin’ over the one fact that kills his whole case: the *ordinary* meaning of sandwich ain’t “any handheld bread situation with meat in it.” If it were, then every calzone with a crust would be in the sandwich witness protection program. The bun on a hot dog ain’t just “a bun”; it’s a specialized vessel built around the dog like a custom suit. A sandwich don’t need a bodyguard from the bread industry — it’s two separate pieces, or at the very least a split that creates two sides. A hot dog is a connected roll opened for convenience, not a sandwich assembled from distinct bread components. That’s not me bein’ fussy; that’s language not goin’ off the rails.

And look, you keep draggin’ in subs and hoagies like they’re your cousins from outta town. Fine, but even there you got the key thing: a sandwich has a recognizable bread structure with bread on two sides of the filling. A hot dog bun is made to cradle *one* specific food in *one* specific form. That’s not a general category; that’s a specialty case, like a glove box built for one glove. You don’t call a tuxedo a “kind of pajamas” just because you can technically wear it and be comfortable-ish, do ya? So no, a hot dog ain’t escaped nothin’ — it never entered the sandwich building in the first place.

[Judge] Round 3: Pro 7/10, Con 8/10
  CON wins by a slim margin because it better grounds the term “sandwich” in ordinary usage, while PRO’s broader functional definition was clever but too elastic for the proposition.

[Judge] Final Verdict (Pro 21 vs Con 23):
Stop. Stop right there. I’ve heard enough.

This whole “hot dog is a sandwich” fight has been dragged through the court like a shopping cart with a busted wheel. And frankly, both sides spent a lot of time dressing up common sense like it was constitutional law.

Here’s the deal:

**Pro** had the cleaner structural argument early on: filling between bread, bun as bread, function over fuss — that’s a decent point. Fine. That’s... actually a decent point. Don’t let it go to your head. But Pro kept leaning on broad definitions and acted like that automatically settled it. It doesn’t. Not in this room. Not with me.

**Con**, on the other hand, did a better job punching holes in Pro’s logic. Con kept returning to the same key distinction: a hot dog bun is not just any bread arrangement; it’s a purpose-built cradle, not a normal sandwich architecture. And yes, that matters. Con also did a better job with the slippery-slope argument — if you define a sandwich too loosely, you end up calling every bread-adjacent lunch a sandwich. That’s not precision; that’s nonsense. Don’t pee on my leg and tell me it’s raining.

The scores already tell the story: **Con 23, Pro 21**. And looking at the rounds, Con edged it by being more consistent and more pointed in rebuttal. Pro had some good language, but good language isn’t a verdict. Con was just a little more convincing, a little more disciplined, and a little less sloppy.

So let’s end this circus.

**WINNER: Con**  
**REASON:** Con made the stronger overall case by showing that a hot dog bun’s structure and purpose distinguish it from a standard sandwich, and Con rebutted Pro’s overly broad “bread plus filling” definition more effectively. Pro had some decent points, but not enough to overcome Con’s tighter reasoning and the higher total score.
