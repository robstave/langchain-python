[Moderator] Proposition:
  "A hot dog is a type of sandwich."

[Pro] Round 1
Well now, if it please the court, I reckon we’re gathered here today to settle a matter as close to the heart of Americana as a front porch swing on a summer’s eve—the humble hot dog and whether it rightfully belongs in the grand family reunion of sandwiches. Now, y’all might be sittin’ there thinkin’ a hot dog’s just a dog in a bun, but I’m fixin’ to show y’all that it’s so much more than that—it’s a bona fide, certified sandwich, bless your heart.

Now, let me spin y’all a little tale. Back when I was knee-high to a grasshopper, my dear departed Mama used to say, “Son, if it looks like a duck, walks like a duck, and quacks like a duck, it’s a duck.” Well, if it’s meat nestled snug as a bug in a bun, served up between bread, it’s a sandwich, no two ways about it. The hot dog fits that bill slicker than owl grease. It’s got the bread, it’s got the filling—be it sausage, chili, onions, or mustard—and it’s meant to be eaten with your hands at a baseball game or a church supper. The National Hot Dog and Sausage Council even calls the bun a “vessel” for the sausage, which is just a fancy way of sayin’ it’s the bread part of a sandwich. Now, if that don't beat all, I don’t know what does!

So I ask y’all, ladies and gentlemen of this fine jury, to look past the name and see the facts plain as day. A hot dog is a sandwich because it meets the very definition of a sandwich—protein between bread. To deny it that status is like sayin’ a hound dog ain’t a dog just ’cause he’s got a bay in his bark. It’s a matter of truth, justice, and good ol’ common sense. I rest my case, and I trust y’all will see it that way too.

[Con] Round 1
Look, uh, hold on a sec. I gotta say, this whole “if it looks like a duck, it’s a duck” thing? Are you serious right now? You’re honestly standin’ there tellin’ me that just ‘cause some meat’s sittin’ in bread, it automatically makes it a sandwich? My Uncle Angie had a thing like dis once — he put sauce on a meatball, stuck it in a bun, called it a “meatball sub.” But you don’t see me callin’ every bun with meat a sandwich, capisce? The hot dog bun ain’t even separate pieces of bread like your classic sandwich — it’s one piece, hinged on the side. That ain’t a sandwich; that’s a different breed of food, like how a calzone ain’t a pizza.

And what’s this “National Hot Dog and Sausage Council” sayin’? You want me to take a council’s word over what regular folks call it on the street? Look, Judge, between you and me, this ain’t about fancy “vessels” or what some folks decide to call it. It’s about the experience, the feel — you don’t see people sittin’ down with a knife and fork to eat a sandwich like they do with a hot dog, right? It’s handheld, casual, and wrapped in a bun that’s designed to cradle the sausage, not sandwich it. So, nah, a hot dog ain’t no sandwich — it’s its own category, a whole ‘nother animal. Oh, and one more thing: you ever try puttin’ ketchup and mustard on a sandwich? Doesn’t feel right, does it? That’s ‘cause a hot dog’s got its own vibe. End of story.

[Judge] Round 1: Pro 5/10, Con 6/10
  The con side’s focus on bun structure and cultural context gave them the edge over the pro’s simplistic “meat in bread” argument.

[Pro] Round 2
Well now, if it please the court, I do declare, my learned friend over there has taken a mighty fine swing, but I’m afraid that dog just won’t hunt. He’s fixin’ to tell y’all that a hot dog ain’t a sandwich ’cause the bun’s hinged and it’s “a whole ’nother animal.” Bless his heart, that’s slicker than owl grease to try and split hairs over the bun’s architecture. I ask y’all, when you pick up a sandwich, be it a BLT or a sloppy joe, ain’t that bread usually connected on one side too? Ever heard of a submarine sandwich, my friends? Bread connected, meat nestled inside, yet we all call that a sandwich without a second thought. So if a submarine sandwich can pass muster, then why not the humble hot dog? It’s the very same principle—protein embraced by bread, meant to be eaten with your hands, enjoyed on a lazy Sunday at the ballpark or a county fair.

Now, about this “experience” and “vibe” my esteemed colleague conjured—well, if I might borrow a line from my dear departed Granddaddy, “Experience don’t trump definition, son.” The way y’all eat a meal might vary, but that don’t change what it is. Folks eat sandwiches all manner of ways, sometimes with a fork and knife, sometimes with their hands, sometimes with a mess of sweet tea on the side. That don’t change the essence of a sandwich, no more than wearin’ overalls makes a man a farmer. And as for ketchup and mustard, well, I’ll be! Y’all ever seen a good ol’ barbecue sandwich? Slathered in sauce, drippin’ down your chin, sittin’ mighty proud between two slices of bread. If that ain’t a kin to the hot dog’s dressin’, I don’t know what is.

So I say to this fine court and all y’all gathered here, let’s not get tangled up in semantics or fancy talk. The hot dog, with its bun cradling that savory sausage, fits snug as a tick on a hound dog right there in the sandwich family. To deny it that place is to deny the very way we understand food and fellowship. And that, folks, is a truth as plain and solid as the cotton fields stretchin’ under a Southern sky. I rest my case, with the hope y’all see the hot dog for what it truly is—a bona fide sandwich, through and through.

[Con] Round 2
Alright, alright, lemme get this straight — you’re tellin’ me a submarine sandwich, with its long roll connected on one side, is a sandwich, but a hot dog bun, also connected on one side, suddenly ain’t? Are you serious right now? That’s like sayin’ a Cadillac’s a car but a Chevy ain’t ‘cause it looks a little different. C’mon, that logic don’t hold water. You’re tryin’ to split buns here, literally, like it’s some kinda magic trick. 

And about that “experience” argument — you say people eat hot dogs with their hands, casual-like, different from sandwiches, which are more “formal.” Look, Judge, between you and me, I’ve eaten plenty of sloppy joes with my hands, and they’re sandwiches by your own reasoning. You’re tryin’ to make it about how people eat it, not what it *is*. That’s like sayin’ a gun ain’t a gun ‘cause you shoot it different than a bow and arrow. The essence don’t change just ‘cause the vibe’s different. Plus, ketchup and mustard on a sandwich ain’t weird — ever had a meatball sub with marinara? Same mess, same sauce situation. 

Oh, and one more thing — if the National Hot Dog and Sausage Council calls the bun a “vessel,” that ain’t some fancy mumbo jumbo; it’s just sayin’ the bun’s doin’ the bread’s job, holdin’ the meat. You can’t just ignore facts ‘cause they don’t fit the story you wanna tell. So, look, this hot dog ain’t some newfangled creature; it’s just a sandwich in a different outfit. End of story.

[Judge] Round 2: Pro 7/10, Con 5/10
  Pro’s argument holds better ground with structural logic and culinary precedent, while Con’s rebuttal falls into contradictory and weak analogies.

[Pro] Round 3
Well now, if it please the court, I’m downright tickled to see my learned friend finally come round to the notion that the bun bein’ connected on one side don’t disqualify a sandwich from sittin’ at the table. As I’ve been tryin’ to say all along, that dog just won’t hunt when you try to make a distinction ‘twixt a submarine sub and a hot dog bun. Both are bread cradlin’ meat, hinged for convenience and ease of eatin’, slicker than a greased pig at a county fair. To say one’s a sandwich and the other ain’t is like sayin’ a hound dog’s a good ol’ dog but a coonhound ain’t just ’cause he’s got a little more snout. If that don’t beat all.

Now, my learned friend would have y’all believe that how folks eat a sandwich or a hot dog somehow changes its very nature. Bless his heart, that notion pains me somethin’ fierce. The truth is, sandwiches are as diverse as the cotton fields in July — some folks eat ’em with their hands, some with silverware, and some folks even eat ’em upside down if the spirit moves ’em. The manner of consumption don’t rewrite the definition no more than wearin’ a Stetson makes a man a cowboy. And as for sauces, well, I reckon if a meatball sub can be drowned in marinara and still be a sandwich, then a hot dog smothered in mustard and onions is just as worthy of that title. It’s all gravy, and that gravy don’t wash away the truth.

So I say to this honorable court and my learned friend, let’s not get tangled in the weeds tryin’ to carve out exceptions where none exist. The hot dog, with its trusty bun holdin’ the sausage snug as a tick to a hound, is as much a sandwich as a catfish is a fish, or a pecan pie is a pie. It’s a matter of honest reckonin’, good sense, and the law of the land as plain as a summer sunrise. I rest my case once more, trustin’ y’all will see the hot dog for the sandwich it truly is — and that, Your Honor, is the gospel truth.

[Con] Round 3
Alright, alright, now hold on a sec — y’all still tryin’ to tell me a hot dog’s just a sandwich wearin’ a different hat? Lemme break it down for ya, straight from the streets of Brooklyn, capisce? This whole “bun’s a vessel” argument? Fuggedaboutit. A vessel’s somethin’ that carries, sure, but it don’t mean it’s a sandwich! You ever seen a canoe carryin’ people down the river? That don’t make the people sandwiches just ‘cause they’re in a boat, right? The hot dog bun ain’t two separate slices o’ bread; it’s one piece folded over, like a taco’s cousin, not that sandwich kind. You can’t just slap the “sandwich” label on somethin’ ‘cause it holds meat.

And this submarine sandwich comparison? C’mon, that’s apples and oranges. A sub’s bread is sliced all the way through — you open it up, you see two distinct bread halves. The hot dog bun? Hinged, baby! It’s like tryin’ to say a calzone’s a pizza ‘cause it’s got the same stuff inside. You eat a calzone different, it’s packaged different — it ain’t no pizza! Same thing here. Folks got a different relationship with hot dogs — you don’t sit down with a napkin and cut ‘em up, you grab it and go, like a street corner hustle. That’s its own category, an identity, like how my Uncle Vinny’s meatballs are different from spaghetti sauce, even though they got the same ingredients.

Oh, and one last thing before I’m done — if how people eat it don’t matter, then why do we never call a burrito a sandwich? It’s bread-like, holds meat, sauce, veggies, and you eat it with your hands! That’s ‘cause common sense tells us it’s a different animal. So, Your Honor, between you and me, this ain’t about fancy words or councils — it’s about respectin’ what stuff really is. A hot dog ain’t a sandwich; it’s a hot dog. Period.

[Judge] Round 3: Pro 6/10, Con 7/10
  Con side edges out with clearer distinctions and better rebuttal, but both sides rely too much on cultural assumptions rather than hard definitions.
